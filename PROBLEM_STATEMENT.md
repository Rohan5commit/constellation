# Problem Statement

## The Core Problem

People save more fragments than they can meaningfully revisit.

A screenshot of a recipe becomes buried in a camera roll. A voice memo about a startup idea gets separated from the class project that inspired it. A reminder to text a friend back gets detached from the feeling that made it important. A note written during burnout sits beside a saved internship link and a travel list, but none of those fragments speak to one another.

The problem is not deletion. The problem is **fragmentation, context loss, and failed resurfacing**.

## Why This Matters

When important fragments fail to return at the right time, people lose more than information:

- ideas go cold
- personal intentions disappear
- relationships drift without notice
- unfinished plans become invisible
- emotional signals get buried under digital clutter

For students and early-career creatives, this creates a subtle but constant friction: they are surrounded by traces of what once mattered, but lack a system that helps them reconnect meaningfully.

## Lost & Found Interpretation

Constellation treats Lost & Found as a question of **memory retrieval, not object recovery**.

The product imagines forgotten digital fragments as drifting through a calm night sky until the user can gather them into constellations. The visual metaphor is not decorative. It directly supports the product premise:

- fragments drift when context is lost
- constellations appear when meaning is restored
- resurfacing works when timing, emotion, and intention align

## Opportunity Space

There is whitespace between existing categories:

- note-taking tools are good at storage but weak at emotional resurfacing
- reminder tools are good at explicit tasks but weak at latent intention
- bookmarking tools preserve links but not why they mattered
- journaling tools preserve reflection but not scattered fragments from everyday digital life
- AI assistants often summarize, but rarely help users rediscover gently and credibly

## Design Challenge

How might we design a system that helps people recover forgotten ideas, plans, people, and moments from scattered digital traces without becoming overwhelming, creepy, or productivity-maximalist?

## Success Criteria

The solution should:

- make the Lost & Found theme obvious from the first screen
- feel emotionally intelligent rather than mechanical
- help users understand why resurfaced fragments matter
- transform scattered inputs into clear, calm next steps
- stay prototype-friendly and credible for a live judging demo
