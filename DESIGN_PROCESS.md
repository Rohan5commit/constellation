# Design Process

## 1. Frame the Right Loss

The first design move was to redefine “lost”. In this concept, people are not losing files permanently. They are losing **the thread that made a fragment meaningful**.

This reframing led to a more interesting question than “How do we organize saved things?”

> How do we help people recover the meaning of what they once cared about?

## 2. Identify the Opportunity

The opportunity sits between capture tools and memory tools.

Existing products help users save, store, tag, search, or set reminders. Fewer products help them:

- reconnect fragments across apps and moments
- revisit emotionally significant material without surveillance-like behavior
- rediscover unfinished intentions at meaningful times

## 3. Explore Concept Directions

Three concept directions were considered:

### Direction A: Search-first memory vault
Useful, but too close to existing note and bookmarking tools.

### Direction B: Timeline scrapbook
Strong emotional tone, but weaker for cross-context pattern detection and live demo novelty.

### Direction C: Constellation-based rediscovery system
Strongest thematic fit, most memorable visual metaphor, clearest narrative arc for judging, and best balance of originality + utility.

Direction C became the final concept.

## 4. Prioritize for Designathon Feasibility

The MVP was selected using four filters:

- emotional clarity
- prototype legibility
- novelty in under 30 seconds
- ability to demo without backend complexity

The resulting scope favors a high-fidelity narrative prototype over feature breadth.

## 5. Prototype Strategy

The product was designed as a route-based web prototype with seeded data because judges need to understand the system quickly.

Key decisions:

- use a **story-rich landing page** to make the theme immediately legible
- use a **seeded demo mode** to guarantee narrative quality during judging
- use **route-based screens** instead of a crowded single dashboard
- keep AI outputs short, careful, and emotionally restrained
- create a **Figma companion prototype** for visual presentation and handoff

## 6. Interaction Design Principles

- calm, premium, and emotionally clear
- no cluttered control panels
- no over-explaining in the UI
- motion used for rediscovery, not spectacle
- every screen understandable within five seconds

## 7. AI Boundary Decisions

AI is intentionally constrained.

It can:

- cluster fragments
- explain possible relevance
- surface reflection prompts
- suggest reconnection next steps

It cannot:

- invent memories
- claim certainty without evidence
- simulate intimate knowledge of the user
- override human judgment

## 8. Research Visibility Strategy

Because this is a designathon, the process must be visible and credible. The repository includes a full research kit and clearly marks where real evidence must replace provisional assumptions.

This makes the team’s design thinking legible even before final interview synthesis is complete.

## 9. Planned Validation Loop

1. Run short interviews with target users who save too many fragments.
2. Validate whether fragmentation and context loss are stronger pain points than simple retrieval.
3. Test whether users understand the constellation metaphor immediately.
4. Observe whether resurfacing feels delightful, neutral, or invasive.
5. Adjust hierarchy, language, and trust cues based on feedback.

## 10. Iteration Logic

If testing reveals confusion, iterate in this order:

- tighten language on landing and onboarding
- simplify the rediscovery feed hierarchy
- clarify “why this resurfaced” explanations
- reduce visual density in the map view
- strengthen trust and privacy cues

## 11. What Judges Should See

The process is designed so judges can quickly understand that this submission is:

- grounded in a real human problem
- original in concept and framing
- deliberate in how it balances AI and trust
- thoughtful in prototype scope and storytelling
